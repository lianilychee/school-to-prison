Context Stories from the experts

Marginalization compounds, and we all contribute

“Multiple marginalization is a... narrative to explain how people can be marginalized by different factors...Those marginalizations can compound, and not just compound one plus one” - Douglas, Author: Disability Criminalization

Students who are part of minorities are often marginalized. Disabled students are put in seperate classrooms and African American students are suspended at a higher rate then their classmates. However when these marginalizations overlap, the students who fall into these segments of the school system are negatively affected.

"All of us, as people wrestle with [deficit thoughts] in our daily lives….It is...blaming the victim rather looking at the larger barriers or larger structures in place that can hinder a student’s performance." –Erin, PHD: Department of Education

Teachers don’t have resources in underfunded schools.

“[Pockets of low income schools] have higher caseloads with less resources...to address challenging behavior. If I’m a teacher in that district, it’s not because I’m a bad teacher, I don’t have the same resources and experience.” -Carol, PHD: Department of Special Education

Teachers in less affluent school districts are not supported in ways to increase their development as effective teachers. In addition school districts in areas with higher levels of poverty are less likely to be able to hire teachers with large quantities of experience. Often this seems to result in classrooms where it is easier to remove a difficult student then it is to support their needs.

“If you don’t have the right kind of professional support, you have good people that leave… I am a teacher that left. It wasn’t about the students, it was about the policy that failed me.” -Carol, PHD: Department of Special Education

“You have really good people leaving because they’re not successful, or you have people that stay and…retire on the job. You have emotionally checked out. I don’t know which is worse.” -Carol, PHD: Department of Special Education

“I'm working in a school right now, a very affluent area, where in every classroom, there is one teacher and...at least one pair professional...That is a resource in and of itself." –Erin, PHD: Department of Education

Disabled students are often excluded instead of supported.

“Students with disabilities,...when they’re having issues communicating, its just perceived as behavior problems. If we look at it from a different perspective, maybe it should really be our responsibility to engage those students in different ways. I’m not suggesting it’s easy, but that’s one of those things that ought to be done. -Erin, PHD: Department of Education

Disabled students are often pulled from the regular classroom into secluded areas. This hurts their development and most students do not require this exclusion. Often for teachers it is easier to suspend or diagnose a student with a disability than it is to keep the student in the classroom. This is particularly true if the teacher does not have the support or experience required to function with these students who have different needs. These students are punished rather than being supported.

"The first step in the school to prison pipeline is when a teacher perceives a student as disruptive or defiant or having some sort of behavior problem. So the teacher will choose to send that kid out to the office with some kind of exclusionary discipline, suspension, maybe expulsion. –Erin, PHD: Department of Education

“I thought that [the justice system of the united states] was more about rehabilitation but what it has become is about punishment. Our culture has developed into a punishment culture and so we have also these disproportionate levels of suspension of black children in schools.” –Oscar, Education Mentor

Solutions are hard, but exist.

“Parent involvement is one of the most difficult and missing links….Parents have tons of rights, especially with respect to disability, to be very involved.” -Carol, PHD: Department of Special Education

Many solutions have been proposed by experts. It is a difficult space as there are practically no words to even discribe these issues. However, just having these discussions can help the students as these ideas are confronted by students, teachers and other individuals.

“People have focused on the war on poverty... very low income, danger inflicted neighborhoods, lower socioeconomic neighborhoods that may have violence going on in them. Nothing has ever worked except one thing in terms of turning around the likelihood that people succeed....integration.” –Oscar, Education Mentor

"Ms. Adams was a really good teacher at getting students engaged, not sending them to the office, setting high expectations."" -Erin, PHD: Department of Education

"School wide positive behavioral support...looks at building a respective positive school climate at the school level. Policies insure that intervention...is looked at in proactive ways instead of reactive ways."" -Erin, PHD: Department of Education
